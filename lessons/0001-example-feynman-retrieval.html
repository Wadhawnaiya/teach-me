Lesson 0001 — Why Retrieval Practice Beats Re-reading

Primary inspiration: Roediger & Karpicke (2006) testing effect + synthesis from Feynman, Khan, Huberman, Oakley.

Re-reading feels productive. It is mostly an illusion of fluency.

The single highest-leverage thing you can do after encountering new material is try to retrieve it from memory without looking.

Quick Feynman Checkpoint

Explain this out loud or in writing as if to a bright 12-year-old who has never heard the term:

“Why does testing yourself (active recall) create much stronger long-term memory than re-reading the same notes?”

Then compare with the source material. Where were the gaps?

Mini Retrieval Practice

Which produces the strongest long-term retention?

A. Re-reading your notes three times
B. Highlighting key sentences while reading
C. Closing the book and writing or speaking what you remember
D. Making the notes look prettier

Struggling to recall is not a sign you are bad at learning. It is the moment your brain is building stronger connections.

Remember your mission. Every small act of retrieval moves you closer. After this lesson: 10–20 minutes of calm NSDR (or a short nap) + protect your sleep tonight.
